Dreyfus model of skill acquisition

Novice > Advanced Beginner > Competent > Proficient > Expert

In 1980, the Dreyfus brothers produced a now-famous model of skill acquisition. You begin as a Novice, and continue on to become an Advanced Beginner, Competent, Proficient and finally, an Expert.

The gist of it is that you move from "dogmatic following of rules and lack of big picture" to "deliberate planning and perceiving deviations from the normal pattern" to "intuitive transcending of rules and envisioning what is possible."

When you start learning a new skill, you're completely incompetent. You begin by mostly being frustrated and unable to progress until someone spoon-feeds you the steps. Or else, as Dreyfus and Dreyfus put it, you "like a baby, pick it up by imitation and floundering".

[breathe]

Then you soon reach a point where the skill becomes possible to practice alone, and you are now excited and working hard. You improve rapidly as you do the easy, reachable parts. Once you complete those, however, the unsustainably rapid pace of improvement levels off, and learning more alone becomes difficult.

Hacker School has a set of Social Rules, and they include the following:

No feigning surprise You shouldn't act surprised when people say they don't know something. This applies to both technical things ("What?! I can't believe you don't know what the stack is!") and non-technical things. This has absolutely no social or educational benefit: people usually do it to boost their own ego. No well-actually's A well-actually happens when someone says something that's almost, but not entirely, correct, and you say, "well, actually…" and then give a minor correction. This is especially annoying when the correction has no bearing on the actual conversation. This doesn't mean Hacker School isn't about truth-seeking or that we don't care about being precise: almost all well-actually's in our experience are about grandstanding, not truth-seeking.

You do have to share your doubts, so others who are doubting themselves know they aren't alone.

You do have to have a lucky attitude, accept opportunities, and work joyously on the task in front of you.

And remember, the tigers don't matter.
