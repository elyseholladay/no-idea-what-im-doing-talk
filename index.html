My tigers are JavaScript, because I haven't had—or made—enough time to learn it well enough.

The tigers don't matter.

But there will always be tigers.

We must forget the tigers waiting to devour us and focus on what we are capable of doing now. We are drawn to challenges without knowing if we can face them. This leads to a fear of trying, a fear of being present, an inability to focus. So we become convinced we can't. Our focus is on the tigers, not the strawberries.

Learning something new is incredibly difficult, especially in tech. There are ten trillion ways to do the same things, tutorials assume you are using some newfangled tool and you can't find anything basic, and god help you if you leave out a semicolon or a bracket or something:

This guy Steve Schwartz wrote a post called "No One Knows What the F*** They’re Doing (or “The 3 Types of Knowledge”)", and in it he talks about learning and impostor syndrome.

He says, "There’s the shit you know, the shit you know you don’t know, and the shit you don’t know you don’t know." (note: this chart is not to scale; the red slice is unimaginably huge.)

"Traditional school would lead you to believe that you must cram as much information as possible into the `shit you know` category."

you don't know” category.

"I am going to be bold and suggest that this is wrong. The goal isn’t to put as much as possible into the "shit you know" category, it’s to take as much as possible out of the "shit you don't know you don't know" category."

He gives an example: Imagine you are a nurse and you need to administer a drug to a patient. If you know that the prescribed drug reacts negatively with another drug the patient is taking, you know not to give it to them.

If you remember that the drug has twenty side effects and tends to react with other drugs, you know to either look it up or ask someone else who knows. The only dangerous scenario could be NOT realizing that there could be any side-effects or reactions.

The only dangerous category is the 'shit you don’t know you don’t know'. The same goes for any profession.

but here's the secret

In dev, or even design, we spend a lot of time just iterating, as we remove items from that category. We feel that we have no clue what we’re doing, yet we see others being confident.

But here’s the secret. Those other people fall into one of two categories:

First, they have figured this out, and thus they deserve it no more (and no less) than we do. They have a very large `shit you know you don’t know` category, and we can only imagine how immense the `shit you don’t know you don’t know` category is.

or, 2) they legitimately think they know everything. Obviously, they do not. These people are usually very egotistical and don’t realize how little they really know. And probably spend a lot of time being pedantic and telling you how wrong you are.

The REAL reason you feel like a fraud is because you have been successful in taking a lot of information out of the "shit you don't know you don't know" category and putting it into the "shit you know you don't know" category. You know of a lot of stuff you don’t know. The good news is that this makes you able to iterate and improve. The bad news is that it also makes you feel mostly dumb and helpless.

Dreyfus model of skill acquisition

Novice > Advanced Beginner > Competent > Proficient > Expert

In 1980, the Dreyfus brothers produced a now-famous model of skill acquisition. You begin as a Novice, and continue on to become an Advanced Beginner, Competent, Proficient and finally, an Expert.

The gist of it is that you move from "dogmatic following of rules and lack of big picture" to "deliberate planning and perceiving deviations from the normal pattern" to "intuitive transcending of rules and envisioning what is possible."

When you start learning a new skill, you're completely incompetent. You begin by mostly being frustrated and unable to progress until someone spoon-feeds you the steps. Or else, as Dreyfus and Dreyfus put it, you "like a baby, pick it up by imitation and floundering".

We need help.

So now we're scared to try anything new, or admit our failures. At this point, we need help.

We need to take pride in our accomplishments, but never consider our opinions as truth, our education complete. We need to collaborate with others and learn new ways of doing things. We need to teach others so that we can learn even more about what we already know by having to explain it to someone.

We are all bad programmers.

First, be lucky.

You know the kind of person about whom you say, "they have all the luck"? They probably work pretty hard at it. Luck depends not just on a lucky thing happening to you, but also identifying it and acting on it. This is entirely internal.

If you’re looking for opportunities, you’ll see them when they come your way. If you're convinced that you can't do something, you'll turn down the offer. Stop lying to yourself about your skills. You don't always know what you can do. The sheer fact that someone asked you to do something or invited you to participate means they believed you would have something valuable to add.

what you can do.

Jina Bolton is one of my industry heroes. Her talks on refactoring inspired not just my work, but my first talk. Last spring we were at a conference in San Francisco. A bunch of the ladies who spoke and attended had brunch together, including Claudina who spoke earlier. After quite a few mimosas, it came up that I did similar work to her, and that conversation ended with Jina and I submitting a joint workshop on refactoring to SassConf. I definitely had a moment like, "wait, you want to do a talk WITH ME!? I'm nobody!"—but we had a great time, and both learned a lot. I would have never guessed I'd get to work with Jina.

Become the kind of person who has a lucky attitude, who can put yourself in situations where you recognize opportunities and create your own luck. Don't be afraid to ask for that opportunity. Make it yours!

Second, set goals.

So you have something you want to do: Try Sass. Redesign your blog. Learn to cook the world's most perfect scrambled eggs. Build an Ember app. Get 60% female speakers at a tech conference, ladies and gents Karolina and the CSSConf Oakland team, damn! [Applause?]

We are made of the words we choose. If we refuse to speak or define our goals, they'll always be imaginary and unattainable.

What is your goal? How will you know when you get there? What steps will you take? Can you define small, attainable tasks that you can actually accomplish?

We don't master JavaScript, or scrambled eggs, or diversity in tech, without taking baby steps.

Kate Matsudaira, who blogs about productivity, leadership, and being awesome, writes, "I make excuses like 'I only have an hour, and so I guess I will wait to start when I have more time.' The reality is, sometimes I will only ever get an hour, or sometimes I just don’t want to start because the work is hard."

There are no shortcuts.

"I want to understand how to use Ember. Today I am going to install it. Next weekend, I am going to read the MVC To Do tutorial. The Tuesday after, I am going to the local JavaScript meetup because there is a talk on getting started with Ember."

You'll have to ask Karolina what she did for this conference, but I can tell you, from my perspective, all she had to do was ask. Did it solve diversity in tech? No. But did she do something amazing and achieve her goal? Yes.

If you don't have small goals, you'll never reach your big one. You cannot jump from Novice to Expert. There are no shortcuts. And if you never define the steps along the way, your goal will only ever be a wish.

Third, work joyously.

We all have heroes. Maybe yours is designer or developer at your company, or one who blogs, or speaks. Or maybe it's someone in a totally different industry. I'll bet, though, that you see their work and go, "wow, they are always putting out blog posts, or cool side projects. I wish I could be that productive!"

My boyfriend, Jeffrey, is totally the domestic, between us. He's constantly doing little tasks around the house. He'll fold a laundry basket of clean socks in between showering and eating breakfast. He makes his bed most mornings. At first, I didn't get it. I'd go, "why not just save it and do all the cleaning/laundry/whatever at once?" But his place is clutter-free and lovely to come home to, and I have a knee-deep pile of clean laundry on my floor.

“Inspiration exists, but it has to find you working.”

Pablo Picasso

It's not magic. You don't get inspired, sit down for an hour, and spit out a perfect conference talk. Your laundry doesn't do itself. Things get done in small, attainable, sometimes tedious tasks, over time. And you can do them with joy, instead of misery.

When I decided I wanted to be more involved in the tech community, the first step I took was to TA a Girl Develop It class. A year went by, in which told anyone who would listen, that I wanted to write and teach a class myself. Then I got my chance. Suddenly, there was a deadline, and work to be done. The night before my class, I practically had fever dreams about all the ways things could go wrong.

A few months later, I gave my first ever Capital-T-Talk on Sass and refactoring at Refresh Austin.

Emboldened, I submitted more talks. I got accepted. I panicked. Rinse and repeat and here I am.

Side note: at this talk, my boss and 2 of my exes were all in attendance, along with Jeffrey, who was taking me on our first date the next night. Absolutely terrifying.

The hardest part of all of this is not standing up here and talking. Everyone assumes it is, because it's public speaking. "Isn't it scary? Are you nervous!? Doesn't traveling suck?" Really, the hard part is forcing myself to work, to sit down on beautiful sunny weekend days to write and edit and iterate, to DO. And then folding some socks when I'm stuck.

Be kind to yourself.

Kate writes, "Many years ago, it was suggested to me to imagine that I was directing such talk at a good friend, rather than at myself. I was horrified: I couldn’t imagine saying such awful things to someone I cared about."

I try to be realistic. Did I skip the cleaning and side project work to lay on the couch and wallow in cookie crumbs and tears? Or did I skip it because I had a long week and truly needed some down time? One is valid. But the other, instead of berating myself, you know, the weekend is over. It does no good to make a Monday even harder than it has to be by starting it in a bad attitude.

Define your fears.

When we are gutsy enough to define our fears, they usually seem silly. Saying your horrible self-doubts aloud helps you realize how toxic it is to think that way. Just getting it out of your infinitely imaginative head helps. If you have a friend you trust, talk to them. Admit to the stuff that plagues you. They will probably have very obvious advice for you.

If you can identify your doubts, you can use that information to your benefit in the future. Do this not so you can avoid those situations that make you doubt, but to find ways to conquer the doubts individually, like an game of Existential Self-Doubt Whack-A-Mole.

Fifth, celebrate your wins.

Defining your goals is important, but what about the "how will you know when you get there?" part?

Remember, "we tend to judge others solely by the end product, while we judge ourselves by the struggle we went through to get there.”

Know what it means to succeed.

If you know what it means to succeed, it makes it hard to later discount your wins.

If you wait until you hit your ultimate goal, have ultimate mastery and never screw anything up, you will never get to celebrate.

I have a friend, Matt Jared, who taught himself to code and admits his beginner-ism, but is also now a successful front-end dev freelancer.

He used to come speak to my students at MakerSquare, and he'd tell them to celebrate their wins. He tells them that he watches the "I am the smartest man alive" clip from Billy Madison every time he does something awesome.

In the book The Power of Habit, they talk about habit loops. A habit loop begins with a cue that triggers a habit. Matt says, "I force the cue when I figure something out. The celebration reinforces the feeling of success, and I know that feeling is coming when I'm in the middle of a struggle."

Do the work to accomplish small tasks along the way to your goal, and be sure to celebrate yourself when you complete them.

And lastly, help others.

I just read an article about start-up entrepreneurs burning out and being unable to admit their fear of failure.

It said, "Rather than showing vulnerability, business leaders have practiced what social psychiatrists call impression management--also known as 'fake it till you make it.'"

Let's go back to the tiger-ignoring, strawberry-focusing story. I'm adding a character. This new guy comes riding in on a tiger to try and get some strawberries. It seemed like a direct route, maybe?

Our heroine looks at him from her cliff and thinks "This guy's really got it together! He's brave!" And the man riding the tiger is thinking, "How the hell did I get on a tiger, and how do I get up there where she is!?"

We are all, simultaneously, riding a tiger, and looking on in awe.

Fake it til you make it?

You have a lot in the 'shit you don't know' category, so you can’t offer certainty—especially not in this industry—without lying. You’re afraid that your colleagues won't accept you unless you know everything. It makes you feel insecure, because you WANT to be accepted by your colleagues and coworkers, but you feel you have to pretend or be dismissed as a dumb, helpless idiot.

We sometimes conflate "fake it til you make it" with going on the defensive when our skills are questioned, or bluffing when asked to do something we are uncomfortable with.

It's not that at all. It's just about accepting where you are in your journey, and being confident anyway.

It's rare that not knowing the answer makes you look like an idiot. What makes you look like an idiot is blundering about making things up as you go along and word-vomiting because your ego is fragile. Nobody worth knowing was ever mean to someone who said, "I don't know, will you help me find out?"
